## Survey Design

<!-- Surveys give you breadth — patterns across a larger sample. Design them AFTER your interviews, so you're quantifying real patterns you've already observed, not testing assumptions you haven't validated.

Key rules:
- No leading questions ("Don't you agree that...")
- No double-barreled questions ("How satisfied are you with X and Y?")
- No loaded framing ("Given the importance of security, how would you rate...")
- Randomize answer order where possible
- Include "Not applicable" or "I don't know" options — forcing a choice creates false data
-->

## Success Criteria

<!-- This is the most important section. Define what "done" looks like BEFORE you start.

Use the "we'd know the answer if..." formulation — it forces you to be specific about what evidence would actually change your mind. If you can't articulate what would make you say "yes, do it" vs. "no, don't" vs. "we need more data," your research questions aren't sharp enough yet. -->

### We'd know the answer if...

<!-- Write concrete conditions. These should be falsifiable — you can look at your data and say definitively whether the condition was met.

Example patterns:

"We'd know [segment] has this problem if at least 6 of 10 interviewees describe [specific behavior] without being prompted."

"We'd know [pricing assumption] is wrong if survey data shows fewer than 30% of respondents have budget authority above [threshold]."

"We'd know [feature] is a real blocker — not just a checkbox — if interviewees can describe a specific instance where it prevented a purchase in the last 12 months."
-->
